Plate XII · cantharellaceae
Conditions favor
Pacific golden chanterelle
Cantharellus formosus

Afruiting flush is likely under Douglas-fir in moist 40–60 year stands, particularly on the north slope above Tahoe Meadows. The duff has now drunk through three storms; humidity reads at 84% with overnight temperatures holding above 8°C.

Look beneath sword fern and salal, in the shade where moss covers fallen needles. The apricot odor is diagnostic; the false gills are blunt forked ridges, never sharp blades. The cap fades from salmon-orange to pale grey-brown with age.

Beware the woolly chanterelle (Turbinellus floccosus), deeply vase-shaped with scaly orange-red interior — causes GI distress.

Plate XII · genus Cantharellus
Choice edible · Mycorrhizal · Western N. America

Pacific
golden chanterelle.

Cantharellus formosus
Also: Pacific chanterelle, golden chanterelle · Common names vary by basin
●  Choice edible
Hand-tinted plate XII Fig. 1

The Pacific golden chanterelle is the signature commercial mushroom of the Pacific Northwest, ectomycorrhizal with conifers in moist coastal and montane forests — especially young-to-mid-seral Douglas-fir and western hemlock stands. It fruits from soil, often hidden under moss, sword fern, salal, and Oregon grape, typically on shaded north-facing slopes.

The cap is yellowish-orange to salmon, fading to grayish-brown with age. Surfaces are dry, finely felted to smooth; convex when young, expanding to flat and then funnel-shaped with wavy inrolled margins. Mature specimens range from two to fourteen centimeters across.

The diagnostic feature: false gills. These are blunt, forked, decurrent ridges with cross-veins — not true gills. The color is orangey-yellow to salmon-pink, paler than the cap, and the structure can be peeled from the cap.

Odor is mild and fruity — reminiscent of apricots. Sustained 1990s studies of commercial harvests show that plucking does not appear to suppress fruiting, though stem-cutting may slightly depress biomass.

The core move

Rather than fattening a phone column, treat the desktop as a published volume — running heads, plate numbers, drop caps, marginalia, deckle-edge dividers, and a persistent Field Clipboard that travels with the reader.

Three columns, always

Left binding (nav + cross-refs), center spread (the content), right Clipboard (live state). Pin a species, change a spot, scrub a chart — the rail reflects it without leaving the page.

Density without noise

Each screen surfaces 4× more data than the mobile equivalent while remaining editorial — multi-column body, marginalia for warnings, KV cards for taxonomic depth, scrub-cursor charts.

Keyboard-first

⌘K opens search. ⌘1–6 swaps screens. J/K moves between cards. F bookmarks. The desktop rewards practiced hands; the mobile rewards a careful walk.
